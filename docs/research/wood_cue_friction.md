# Anchor 7 — Cue Restructuring + Friction Design (Wood)

## Research Memo (v1 — pending Codex verify)

**One-line summary:** Habits form because **stable contexts repeatedly cue the same behavior**, not because of conscious decision. Behavior change therefore depends more on changing the environment (cues + friction) than on strengthening willpower. Willpower fails predictably under fatigue, stress, and decision fatigue; environment design works persistently because it removes the moment of decision.

**The framework.** Wendy Wood and colleagues (Wood & Rünger 2016, *Annual Review of Psychology*; Wood 2019, *Good Habits, Bad Habits*):

- **Habits = context-cued automatic responses.** A behavior repeated in a stable context becomes triggered by the context itself, with little or no conscious deliberation. The bedroom + nighttime + phone-in-hand combination cues scrolling before any decision is made.
- **Friction shapes which behaviors survive.** Behaviors with low friction (one tap away, in sight, prepared in advance) win against high-friction behaviors. People often misread their own behavior as "weak motivation" when it's actually "high friction."
- **Cue restructuring is more effective than willpower.** Removing the old cue (phone out of bedroom) or installing a new cue (workout clothes by the door) changes behavior more reliably than promising to "be more disciplined."
- **Habit discontinuity** (Verplanken & Wood 2006): life transitions (moving, changing jobs, new semester) disrupt cue patterns and create unusually good windows for habit change.
- **Old cues retain power.** Even after a habit weakens, returning to old contexts can reactivate it. This explains relapse after vacations, moves, etc.

**Key empirical findings:**
- Lally et al. 2010 — average ~66 days for habit automaticity in stable context; massive variation; depends on context stability and repetition consistency.
- Multiple intervention studies show environment-design interventions (default settings, friction insertion) outperform self-reported intention changes for sustained behavior change.
- "Knowing better" doesn't translate to action when cues remain intact (Wood & Neal 2007).

**Non-obvious operational details:**
1. **Willpower is a finite, time-of-day-sensitive resource.** Late at night, after stress, after decision fatigue, willpower approaches zero. Environment design works at all times because it doesn't require willpower at the moment of action.
2. **Friction works because it inserts 5-10 seconds of hesitation.** Not prevention. The 5 seconds creates space for deliberate control to enter. Removing the phone from the bedroom doesn't make scrolling impossible; it adds steps that break automaticity.
3. **Cue restructuring is more powerful than friction-on-old-behavior.** Removing the cue entirely (phone out of room) > making the cue harder (phone in drawer). But friction is cheaper to deploy and works when full cue removal isn't possible.
4. **Visible cues for desired behavior are as important as removed cues for undesired behavior.** Workout clothes by the door is the *positive cue* equivalent of phone-out-of-bedroom. Many people only do half (remove bad cues without installing good cues) and wonder why nothing fills the gap.
5. **The willpower-frame failure is identity-protective.** When Alex says "I just need to be more disciplined," he's framing failure as character. Reframing to environment ("the cue structure was set up to fail") reduces shame and opens up actionable changes.

**Anti-patterns ruled out:**
- "Just be more disciplined" / "you need willpower" — predictably fails late at night / under stress
- Removing bad cues without installing good ones — leaves a vacuum the old behavior fills
- Treating friction as prevention rather than hesitation insertion — sets up disappointment when the friction is overcome
- Ignoring habit discontinuity windows — moving / new semester / new job are the cheapest moments to install new habits, often missed

**NOUS-side dual-use line:** Same as Anchor 6 — this anchor is for helping Alex *redesign his own environment*. NOT a framework NOUS should apply to itself for retention engineering (variable rewards, friction reduction, cue installation in the user's day are the same mechanisms social media exploits). Surface only when Alex frames change as willpower-driven.

**Interaction with other anchors:**
- **Function replacement (Anchor 6)**: complementary. Function tells you WHAT substitute satisfies the need; cue/friction tells you HOW the substitute survives in environment over time. Without function, environment design substitutes nothing useful. Without cue/friction, the substitute behavior loses to the cued original.
- **Reis** (responsiveness): if Alex frames willpower failure with shame ("I'm so weak"), Reis U+V+C lands first. Then cue/friction reframe.
- **Watkins** (rumination): if Alex repeats "I should just stop X" abstractly without progress = unconstructive RT. Cue/friction reframe is one of the concrete-shift options.
- **Kross** (self-distancing): rarely needed for cue/friction conversations; this is more cognitive than emotional.

**Sources:**
- Wood, W., & Rünger, D. (2016). *Psychology of habit.* Annual Review of Psychology, 67, 289-314.
- Wood, W. (2019). *Good Habits, Bad Habits: The Science of Making Positive Changes That Stick.*
- Lally, P., van Jaarsveld, C. H., Potts, H. W., & Wardle, J. (2010). *How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world.* European Journal of Social Psychology, 40(6), 998-1009.
- Verplanken, B., & Wood, W. (2006). *Interventions to break and create consumer habits.* Journal of Public Policy & Marketing, 25(1), 90-103.
